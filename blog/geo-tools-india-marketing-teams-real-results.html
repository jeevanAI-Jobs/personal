Which GEO Tools Are Indian Marketing Teams Actually Using in 2026?

The GEO tools category has a content problem: most of the coverage is sponsored, PR-driven, or produced by the tools themselves. Indian B2B marketing teams searching for honest recommendations about which AI visibility platforms are actually working — not just which ones have the biggest marketing budgets — are right to be frustrated. This is what I actually see in the market.

The Honest Starting Point: Most Teams Are Still Manual

The most accurate picture of how Indian marketing teams are doing GEO monitoring in 2026 is: most of them are still doing it manually, or not at all.

The workflow looks like this: a marketing manager or founder opens ChatGPT and Perplexity once a month, submits five to ten queries related to their category, notes whether their brand appears, and makes a mental note to "do something about it." The results live in a Notion page or a Slack message that nobody acts on systematically.

This is not a failure of the teams doing it. It reflects the gap between what the current crop of GEO tools offers and what Indian B2B marketing teams at seed to Series A stage actually need. The tools that exist either have enterprise pricing built for global markets, limited query volumes that do not cover a team's full query landscape, or reporting layers that are impressive but not connected to content action.

The result is that the most common GEO "tool" Indian marketing teams use is a combination of: Perplexity itself for spot checks, Google Search Console for AI Overviews impression data, and whatever time the marketing manager has to do manual citation checks each month.

What Teams That Are Using Paid Tools Are Actually Getting

Indian B2B teams that have committed to paid GEO monitoring tools tend to fall into two categories: those that have built a content action workflow around the data, and those that have a reporting dashboard that nobody acts on.

The first group sees results. Teams that track 100 or more queries across at least three AI engines, assign a specific person to review the data monthly, and produce two to four content pieces per month based on the gap analysis report measurable improvement in Perplexity and Google AI Overviews citation frequency within 60 to 90 days. The tool is the input to the workflow, not the outcome.

The second group pays for a tool and waits for visibility to improve. It does not improve. The tool shows them that they are absent from forty queries. Nobody publishes any content to address those gaps. The monthly report looks the same three months in a row. The tool gets cancelled.

The differentiating factor is not which tool you use. It is whether your team has a clear owner for acting on the data the tool produces. A mid-tier tool with a clear content workflow outperforms the best tool with no workflow every time.

Global Tools vs Indian-Built Options

The global GEO tools are technically strong. Peec AI, Otterly, and Profound are well-built products with good coverage and useful features. The problem for Indian B2B teams is pricing. These tools are priced for European and US markets. When those prices are converted to INR, the resulting monthly cost puts them in an enterprise-tier category for Indian startup budgets, even when they are mid-tier pricing in their home markets.

Indian-built GEO monitoring tools are emerging but the category is young. The trade-off is currently: global tools with better features and harder-to-justify pricing, versus Indian tools with more appropriate pricing and features that are still catching up to the global options.

For Indian B2B teams, the practical decision framework is: if your primary markets are international and your competitors are global brands, the global tools may be worth the pricing premium because you need coverage of how your brand appears in global query contexts. If your primary market is India and your competition is Indian-market brands, an Indian-built tool at INR pricing with sufficient query coverage and engine breadth is the right call.

What Good GEO Tool Usage Actually Looks Like

The Indian marketing teams getting real results from GEO tools share a specific set of operational habits.

They define their query set before subscribing. Rather than letting the tool suggest queries, they start with the twenty to thirty queries their buyers are most likely asking AI models and build from there. This ensures the tool is measuring what matters to the business, not what the tool's algorithm thinks is important.

They track competitors systematically. Not just "are we appearing" but "when we do not appear, which competitors are appearing instead, and what content are they using." This turns gap analysis from a passive observation into an active content brief.

They assign ownership explicitly. One person is responsible for the monthly GEO report and for converting the findings into two content actions per month. Without named ownership, the data sits in a dashboard and nothing changes.

They separate GEO from SEO reporting. Teams that combine GEO data with SEO data into a single report often end up prioritizing the SEO metrics because they are more established and easier to explain to stakeholders. GEO metrics need to stand alone in reporting until they are understood well enough to integrate.

The Features That Actually Get Used

Among teams using paid GEO tools, the features that are actually used versus the features that are paid for but ignored break down clearly.

Used consistently: citation frequency by query (how often the brand appears), competitor citation frequency for the same queries, month-over-month change tracking.

Used sometimes: sentiment analysis of how the brand is described, content gap recommendations, citation source tracking.

Paid for but rarely used: API access, custom dashboard integrations, white-label reporting, real-time alerts, unlimited query banks beyond the first 100.

This means most Indian B2B marketing teams are effectively using only a fraction of what they are paying for in enterprise-tier tools. Mid-tier tools that cover the first two categories well are a better operational fit for most teams at this stage.

What to Actually Do If You Are Starting From Zero

If you are an Indian B2B marketing team starting GEO monitoring from zero, here is the practical sequence:

Month 1: Manual audit only. Define 25 queries. Submit to ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews. Record results in a spreadsheet. Identify your top five gap clusters. Publish two content pieces targeting those gaps. This costs nothing except time and gives you real baseline data before you spend on a tool.

Month 2: If the manual audit confirms meaningful gaps (which it almost certainly will), evaluate a mid-tier tool in the ₹5000 to ₹8000 range. Look specifically for: at least 100 tracked queries, three or more engines, citation source tracking, and competitor benchmarking. Assign a specific owner for acting on the monthly output.

Month 3 onwards: Run the workflow. Two to four content pieces per month based on the gap data. Monthly report to track progress. Adjust query set quarterly as your category evolves.

The teams that are actually getting results from GEO tools are not the ones with the most sophisticated monitoring setup. They are the ones that started early, built a simple workflow, and stuck to it consistently.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which GEO and AI visibility tools are Indian marketing teams actually using in 2026?

Based on my experience with Indian B2B marketing teams, the tools most commonly in use in 2026 are: Perplexity itself (used manually to spot-check brand appearances), Google Search Console (for understanding which queries are driving AI Overview impressions), a small number of Indian-built GEO monitoring tools at sub-₹8000 price points, and global tools like Peec AI and Otterly for teams with larger budgets or international operations. Many Indian marketing teams are still doing manual citation audits — submitting queries to ChatGPT and Perplexity in a spreadsheet — because they have not found a tool that fits their budget and workflow at the same time.

What results are Indian B2B companies seeing from GEO tools?

Indian B2B companies that commit to a structured GEO workflow (monthly citation tracking plus two to four content pieces per month based on the data) typically see measurable improvement in Perplexity and Google AI Overview citation frequency within 60 to 90 days. The clearest results come from companies that publish FAQ-rich content targeting specific query clusters where they were previously absent. Teams that use GEO tools only for reporting without acting on the content gap data see little improvement regardless of which tool they use. The tool is not the lever — the content is.

Are manual GEO audits (without a paid tool) worth doing for Indian startups?

Yes, manual GEO audits are worth doing for Indian startups that are not yet ready to commit to a paid tool. The process is: define 20 to 30 queries your buyers are likely asking AI models, submit each query to ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews, record whether your brand appears, and note which competitors appear when you do not. This takes two to three hours per month and gives a working picture of your citation gaps. The limitation is scale: 20 to 30 queries is enough to identify the highest-priority gaps but not enough to build a comprehensive content strategy. Manual audits are a starting point, not a sustainable operating model for teams that take AI visibility seriously.

What should Indian marketing teams look for in a GEO tool beyond citation tracking?

Beyond basic citation tracking, Indian marketing teams should look for: citation source tracking (which pages AI models are using as sources, not just whether the brand appeared), sentiment analysis (how the brand is being described in AI responses, not just whether it appears), content gap recommendations (which query clusters the brand is missing from and what content would address them), and competitor benchmarking (how often competitors appear for the same queries). Teams that have these four features can turn GEO monitoring into a direct input to their content calendar. Teams without them are running a vanity dashboard.

Which GEO tool mistakes do Indian B2B marketing teams most commonly make?

The most common GEO tool mistakes Indian B2B marketing teams make are: tracking too few queries (under 30) to identify meaningful patterns, using only one AI engine in monitoring (typically ChatGPT) while missing Perplexity where many of their buyers actually research, paying for a tool but not assigning ownership of acting on the data to a specific person, conflating impressions data from Google Search Console with AI citation data (they measure different things), and switching tools too quickly when results do not appear within the first month. AI visibility improvements compound over time. A consistent workflow with any reasonable tool outperforms tool-switching with the best tool.
