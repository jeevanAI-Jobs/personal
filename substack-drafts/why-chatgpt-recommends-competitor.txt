Your product is better. ChatGPT still picks your competitor.

What keeps showing up when I audit brands that are losing AI recommendations, and the content gaps behind it.

- - -

Hi,

I want to share the most uncomfortable pattern I keep running into.

I have been running competitive AI audits across brands in six different industries. The brand that is losing AI recommendations usually has the better product. The brand winning is not better built. It has published more specific, structured content that maps directly to how buyers actually decide.

Here is the scenario I see play out constantly. A buyer in your target market opens ChatGPT and types "what is the best [your category] for [your use case]." Your competitor's name appears. Yours does not. You rank higher on Google, you have stronger reviews, you have a better product. None of it matters at that moment.

And this is no longer a fringe behaviour. According to G2's 2026 Answer Economy report, 51% of B2B software buyers now begin research in an AI chatbot more often than on Google, up from 29% a year earlier. 69% of those buyers ended up choosing a different vendor than they originally planned, based on AI guidance. One in three bought from a brand they had never heard of before opening the chatbot.

The problem is not that AI is unfair. It is that AI is making a specific judgment about your brand using signals you have not optimised for yet.

**How AI actually decides**

AI search platforms do not simply retrieve the highest-ranking website. They synthesise evidence across the whole web to score each brand on the buying signals relevant to the query. A brand ranking #8 on Google with clearly documented use cases, third-party citations, and specific outcome data gets recommended over a #1-ranked brand with vague positioning.

Every recommendation engine, whether ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity, is trying to answer one question: given what this buyer needs, which brand can I most confidently point them to? To answer it, AI looks for evidence. Not mentions. Evidence: specific claims about what the product does, for whom, with what results, validated by sources outside your own website.

**The gaps that cost you the recommendation**

In audit after audit, four content gaps explain most of the invisibility:

1. **No use-case-specific pages.** "We help businesses grow" matches no real query. Your competitor has a page for each buyer type you both serve.

2. **Outcome data is vague or missing.** "Customers see results" is invisible. "Customers cut onboarding time by 40%" is a citable claim.

3. **Third-party citations are thin.** AI trust is built externally. If only your own site says you are good, AI treats that as unverified. AirOps found that 85% of brand mentions in AI answers come from external third-party domains, not the brand's own website.

4. **No FAQ or structured Q&A content.** FAQ sections are among the highest-cited content types across every major AI platform. Brands without them lose citation share on every query in their category.

**What to do this week**

The sequence matters more than the volume. A generic "what is [category]" post will not move anything. One specific page that addresses the exact use case your competitor is winning on can start moving your citation rate within 4 to 6 weeks.

Start with your single lowest signal. If it is use-case fit, publish one detailed page per core buyer segment that names the problem, the workflow, and a quantified outcome. If it is trust, go earn third-party citations and review placements, because AI cannot cite sources that do not exist.

And measure it properly. Run the same structured query set before and after you publish, so you are tracking a delta rather than a snapshot. Across the audits I have run, brands that make the right content changes typically move from a 28 to 35% baseline citation rate to 55 to 65% within 8 to 12 weeks of consistent publishing.

One last thing worth sitting with: BrightEdge research shows the overlap between Google's top-10 rankings and AI citations has collapsed from 75% in mid-2025 to between 17% and 38% by early 2026. High rankings no longer deliver AI visibility on their own. The goal now is to rank and get cited.
